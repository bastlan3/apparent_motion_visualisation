Transformational Apparent Motion

Four classic paradigms that probe how the visual system interpolates between two flashed shapes. When timing is right, observers see a single object transforming — sliding, rotating, growing, curving — rather than two disconnected flashes. Each paradigm pulls apart a different rule governing that interpolation.

von Schiller, P. (1933). Stroboskopische Alternativversuche. Psychologische Forschung, 17, 179–214.

Predicted percept

— vertical motion
A correspondence-by-proximity rule (Korte's law of distance) predicts that motion is seen along the shorter diagonal-pair distance. With a vertical bias of ≈ 0.85 on the aspect ratio (a known asymmetry in the human display), the cross-over from horizontal to vertical percept happens just below AR = 1.

About this paradigm

Four dots sit on the corners of a rectangle. The NW + SE diagonal flashes, then the NE + SW diagonal, alternating. The visual system must match each dot to a partner in the next frame, but two matchings are geometrically valid: horizontal pairing (NW→NE & SE→SW) or vertical pairing (NW→SW & NE→SE).

Schiller showed the percept is governed by the inter-flash distance: the shorter pair wins. With a square layout the display is bistable and alternates spontaneously. The paradigm became a cornerstone of the correspondence problem in motion vision.
Shepard, R. N., & Judd, S. A. (1976). Perceptual illusion of rotation of three-dimensional objects. Science, 191(4230), 952–954.
Two views of a rigid 3-D object, alternating —
When the SOA is long enough, observers report rigid rotation. The threshold SOA grows linearly with angular disparity — equally fast for depth and picture-plane rotations.

About this paradigm

A small asymmetric 3-D object is shown alternately in two orientations differing by θ. With short SOA the two views appear as separate flashes; with long SOA the visual system bridges them with a perceived rigid rotation. Plotting the minimum SOA that still yields rigid rotation against θ gives a straight line with essentially identical slopes for in-depth and in-plane rotations. This linearity was the empirical lift-off for treating apparent motion as a kinematic interpolation.

About this paradigm

The shape — here an asymmetric arrow — alternates between two positions and orientations. The motion is bridged by a curved path rather than a straight line. To estimate the path, McBeath & Shepard placed a narrow vertical "window" between the two flashes and asked observers to set its height so the shape was seen smoothly passing through it. The optimal setting traces where the visual system places the path, and it agrees with the bulge predicted by kinematic geometry (the unique smooth rigid screw motion between the two poses).
Foster, D. H. (1975). Visual apparent motion and some preferred paths in the rotation group SO(3). Biological Cybernetics, 18, 81–89.

Apparent-motion path as a variational problem —
Foster's idea: the perceived motion path minimises an action functional ∫ L(x, ẋ) dt. Different cost functions predict different paths.
Energy along the path

Cost functional

Euclidean: minimise ∫|ẋ|² → straight line in position+orientation coordinates (Scheme 1 in Foster 75).
Screw: minimise ∫(ω² + αv²) on SE(2) → one-parameter subgroup (Scheme 2 in Foster 75 — the bi-invariant geodesic).
Smoothness: minimise ∫|ẍ|² → minimum-jerk-like curved path.

About these models

Foster (1975) framed apparent motion as choosing a path through configuration space — for a tilting bar this is SO(3). He compared two schemes generated by different metrics, and observers favored the bi-invariant geodesic (Scheme 2) on 6 of 8 conditions.

Foster (1978) extended this to a calculus of variations: the visual system chooses paths minimising an action functional. Changing the integrand changes the predicted path — this is what the toggle on the left lets you explore.
